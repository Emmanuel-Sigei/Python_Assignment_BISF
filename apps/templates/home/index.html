Our history

Our History is a testament to the power of individuals coming together with a shared purpose. From those humble beginnings, we have cast a light on the darkness of GBV, providing hope and healing, and reminding us all that collective action can create lasting change.

In a world marred by the silent suffering of countless individuals, a spark of compassion and determination ignited our remarkable journey. We set out on a mission to raise awareness, empower survivors, and instigate change.

In those early days, our modest gatherings were fueled by hope, but our hearts brimmed with empathy. We engaged in open conversations about the pervasive nature of GBV. and this became the building blocks of our vision, inspiring others to join our cause. With every voice that joined our chorus, our influence grew, and the ripple effect began to reach farther than we could have ever imagined.

Over the years, our organization's tireless efforts expanded its reach across communities, borders, and cultures. Together, we organized workshops, created educational resources, and initiated campaigns that tackled the root causes of GBV. Our impact was felt on an individual level, as survivors found solace and support, and on a societal level, as policies were reshaped and awareness reached new heights.

What impact does GBV have on society?

Gender-Based Violence is a pervasive issue that extends far beyond its immediate victims. Its impact resonates throughout society, touching various aspects of our collective well-being:

GBV inflicts severe psychological trauma on survivors, leading to anxiety, depression, and post-traumatic stress disorder. This suffering affects not only individuals but also their families, friends, and communities who bear witness to their pain.

GBV has been recognized as a public health crisis, demanding resources and interventions that could otherwise be invested in proactive health initiatives.

Children who witness or experience GBV are more likely to perpetuate or become victims of violence in the future, perpetuating a cycle of abuse that harms society across generations.

GBV diminishes the potential of individuals who could otherwise contribute meaningfully to society. When survivors are held back, our collective human capital suffers.
